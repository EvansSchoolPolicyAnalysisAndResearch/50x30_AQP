HOW TO USE THE ‘50x30 CAMBODIA DATA EXPLORER’

Welcome to the ‘50x30 Cambodia Data Explorer’, an agricultural data visualization tool. On this tab of the web application, we provide step-by-step instructions, tips, and frequently asked questions (FAQs) about the application.

STEP-BY-STEP INSTRUCTIONS:

  1. CLICK ‘BROWSE DATA’ AT TOP

  1. SELECT A POLICY PRIORITY

Are you interested in exploring data related to poultry, livestock, or crop production? Select the category of data you are interested in exploring.

  1. SELECT INDICATOR

Click the drop down box to browse available indicators, or statistics from the Cambodia Inter-Censual Agricultural Survey. Note that you can visualize up to two indicators at one time (see step 4 CHOOSE CORRELATE).

As you select indicators, the ‘Survey Question’ box will give you information about the survey question or survey questions the indicator represents.

  1. (OPTIONAL) CHOOSE CORRELATE

Click the drop down box to browse a correlate (secondary indicator) if you are interested in browsing data for two indicators at one time.  

  1. (OPTIONAL) OMIT 0s FROM THE INDICATOR

Policymakers may optionally choose to omit 0s from the statistics for multiple reasons such as enhancing data visualization or restricting observations to households who actually engaged in activities. See FAQs for more information about cases where you may want to omit 0s from your data visualization.

  1. SELECT ADMINISTRATIVE LEVEL

        Select either ‘Province’ or ‘Household’.

  • Select ‘Province’ if you are interested in exploring averages by province.
  • Select ‘Household’ if you are interested in exploring averages by household.

  1. SELECT GROUPING VARIABLE

Select one grouping variable if you are interested in disaggregating data by household characteristics.

  • Select ‘None’ if you are not interested in disaggregating data by household characteristics.
  • Select ‘Male/Female Household Head’ to disaggregate data by head of household gender.
  • Select ‘COVID Impact’ to disaggregate data by whether or not a household reported being impacted by COVID or not.
  • Select ‘Association Membership’ to disaggregate data by households belonging to formal or informal farmers’ communities/associations or not.
  • Select ‘Farm Size’ to disaggregate data by households by total farm size measured in hectares (ha).

  1. VISUALIZE THE DATA

If you only select an indicator and not a correlate, you will see two figures: a histogram and a geographical map. only one histogram and one geographical map.

If you select both an indicator and a correlate, you will see five figures: a histogram for each indicator, a map for each indicator, and a scatter plot representing the relationship between the indicators.

  1. DOWNLOAD THE FIGURES

Right click the image. Select ‘Save Image As’. Choose a descriptive name for a figure. See TIPS about how to choose descriptive figure names.

TIPS:

  1. Download and save generated figures (histograms, maps, scatter plots) with a descriptive name

As you download multiple figures to use in presentations and documents, we recommend saving them with a descriptive name that can be easily referenced later. For example ‘histogram_numberofchickens_byhousehold_omit0s’.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS:

My screen is grey with an error message in the bottom left corner that says “Disconnected from Server. Reload.”. What should I do?

Click “Reload” in the error message or refresh the page. You will now have to re-select your indicators and correlates.

What is an indicator?

Indicators are statistics used to measure current conditions and future conditions. Indicators can be summary statistics based on direct questions from the survey or they can be constructed based on multiple survey questions. For example, the indicator ‘Number of chickens sold (live)’ comes directly from the survey question ‘S4_Q15d’ on the Cambodia Inter-Censual Agricultural Survey. Policymakers may choose to select this indicator because they are interested in understanding how the number of live chickens sold per household varies across regions in Cambodia.

Why do I have limited options for indicator selection?

These indicators are statistics that are most closely related to (or indicative of) the policy priority of interest. For example, if you are interested in increasing production and consumption of domestic poultry, the indicator ‘Number of chickens sold (live)’ is a good indicator for the current supply being produced at the household level.

Why do the lists of correlates change depending on the indicator I select?

The unique list of correlates for each indicator has been pre-determined by EPAR based on plausible relationships between indicators in the literature. See the ‘Literature’ tab for more information about relationships between indicators.

Why would I want to check ‘Omit 0s from the Indicator’?

Policymakers may choose to omit 0s from the indicator for multiple reasons.

To enhance data visualization: Some histograms will be heavily skewed with observations with zero values. Checking ‘Omit 0s from the Indicator’ might improve the visualization of the histogram in these instances.

To restrict observations to households that engaged in a particular activity: Imagine that a policymaker is interested in comparing the ‘Number of chickens sold (live)’ across regions. The policymaker may choose to visualize this data in two different ways. Omitting the 0s will allow the policymaker to view the average ‘Number of chickens sold (live)’ across regions among households that actually sold chickens. Alternatively, including 0s will allow a policymaker to view the average ‘Number of chickens sold (live)’ across regions among all households, whether they are engaged in selling chickens or not.

It is important to note that zeroes are different from missing observations. For example, a household that does not answer the survey question “In the past year, how many chickens did you sell?” is different from a household that answers 0 for that same question. ‘Omit 0s from the Indicator’ simply restricts observations to households that both answered the question and provided an answer that was not 0.

What is a histogram?

A histogram is a visual representation of the distribution of numeric data.

What is a scatter plot?

Scatter plots are data visualizations that show relationships between variables.

How should I interpret the histograms generated by this app?

The x-axis displays the range of values that an indicator takes on in the data. The y-axis represents the frequency of those values in the data. Higher bars represent higher frequencies of observations that take on a specific value.

How should I interpret the maps generated by this app?

All maps present province-level, not household-level, averages. Darker colors correspond to provinces that have relatively high average indicator values compared to other provinces.

How should I interpret the scatter plots generated by this app?

The original indicator you selected will always be on the y-axis. The correlate you select will always be on the x-axis. The scatter plot plots household or regional averages of both indicators simultaneously. The line that runs through them is a “best fit line”, indicating whether or not the indicator and correlate have a positive or negative relationship. If the relationship is positive, the indicator will generally increase in value as the correlate increases in the value. If the relationship is negative, the indicator will generally decrease in value as the correlate increases in value.
